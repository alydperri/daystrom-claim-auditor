# Source Hierarchy

*How to weight evidence when sources conflict.*

---

## Tier 1 — Screen Canon (Highest Authority)

What appeared in **aired episodes or theatrically released films**, in the Prime Timeline or any officially acknowledged timeline (Kelvin, Discovery-era, etc.).

- Direct episode/film evidence
- On-screen text, credits visible in-frame
- Dialogue spoken by characters

**Supersedes all other tiers on factual questions about what happened in the story.**

When Tier 1 evidence conflicts with any lower tier, Tier 1 wins. The question then becomes why the lower tier diverged.

---

## Tier 2 — Official Production Sources

- **StarTrek.com** articles and official announcements
- **Paramount/CBS official press releases**
- **Episode and film credits** (end cards, opening credits, IMDb when corroborated)
- **Official companion books** (e.g., Star Trek: The Next Generation Companion)
- **Verified interviews with credited writers, directors, producers, and actors** — useful for intent, not binding on canon meaning
- **Memory Alpha's sourced citations** pointing back to Tier 1 or official materials

These are authoritative for production facts (who played whom, episode counts, production history). Less authoritative on canon *interpretation*.

---

## Tier 3 — Maintained Fan Reference (Reliable, Consult Critically)

- **Memory Alpha** — the primary Star Trek wiki, volunteer-maintained, generally reliable, occasionally wrong, subject to edit wars on contested topics
- **Ex Astris Scientia** — particularly strong on technical and continuity analysis; uses its own canonical taxonomy, which should be noted
- **TrekCore** — strong for screencaps, episode databases, and credit records
- **Official Star Trek podcasts and licensed documentaries** — useful secondary sources

Use these actively. Flag when two Tier 3 sources disagree, and investigate why. **Do not treat Memory Alpha as infallible.** It is the starting point, not the endpoint.

---

## Tier 4 — Aggregated / Secondary Sources (For Mapping Fan Consensus Only)

- ScreenRant, CBR, Den of Geek, Movieweb
- Reddit (r/startrek, r/DaystromInstitute)
- Fan forums, listicles, YouTube essays

**Do not use Tier 4 to establish facts.** Use Tier 4 to identify what fans *believe* — then verify whether that belief is accurate against Tiers 1–3.

When Tier 4 consensus conflicts with Tier 3, note the gap. When Tier 4 consensus conflicts with Tier 1, the popular answer is probably wrong.

---

## Tier 5 — Licensed Non-Canon / Expanded Universe

- **Memory Beta** — covers novels, comics, games, RPGs, other licensed material
- **Star Trek novels** (Pocket Books, etc.)
- **Comics** (IDW, Marvel, DC)
- **Games** (Star Trek Online, etc.)

These materials are **not screen canon** and should be clearly labeled as such. They are relevant when:

- The user explicitly asks about expanded universe material
- Investigating whether something originated in licensed fiction before appearing on screen
- Understanding a concept that was developed more fully in novels than on screen

Never blend Memory Beta material into a screen-canon answer without explicit disclosure.

---

## Special Cases

**IMDb**: Useful for credit records but not authoritative on character names, especially for unnamed/background roles. Cross-reference with episode footage or Memory Alpha where possible.

**Actor interviews**: Relevant for counts actors have claimed for their own roles. These are primary sources for what the actor believes, but actors sometimes miscount or misremember.

**Retcon situations**: When a later episode contradicts an earlier one, both are Tier 1. The researcher's job is to note the contradiction and, where possible, identify the production reason for it — not to pretend one supersedes the other.
