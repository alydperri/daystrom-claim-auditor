# Canon Boundaries

*A working map of what counts, what doesn't, and what's genuinely contested.*

---

## The Core Problem

Star Trek has no single official canon policy document. Unlike Star Wars (which explicitly distinguishes "Legends" from current canon), Paramount/CBS has never issued a comprehensive statement defining the canon hierarchy. This means canon boundaries are partly inferred from producer statements, partly from fan convention, and partly from common sense about what the franchise treats as binding.

The researcher applies a working taxonomy — not a franchise-official one.

---

## Tier A — Screen Canon (Core)

Everything aired or theatrically released in the Prime Timeline:

- TOS (including the two pilots)
- TAS *(see note below)*
- TNG
- DS9
- VOY
- ENT
- Short Treks *(see note below)*
- DIS
- PIC
- SNW
- LD (Lower Decks)
- PRO (Prodigy)
- All theatrically released films: TMP, TWOK, TSFS, TVH, TFF, TUC, GEN, FC, INS, NEM

**TAS Note**: For decades treated as optional or non-canon by many fans and some producers. Current showrunner consensus and recent official acknowledgments treat it as canon. SNW referenced Chapel's TAS appearance. This is now the working assumption.

**Short Treks Note**: Produced as supplements to DSC/SNW. Some establish canonical details; others are comedic or experimental. Treat as canon-adjacent; flag when a Short Treks claim would not be supported by mainline series evidence alone.

---

## Tier B — Kelvin Timeline (Parallel Canon)

The reboot film trilogy (ST 2009, Into Darkness, Beyond) takes place in an alternate timeline explicitly established as such in screen canon (Nero's incursion, Spock Prime's presence).

- **Is it canon?** Yes — the timeline split is a canonical event
- **Does it inform Prime Timeline facts?** No — it's a separate history
- **Does it invalidate Prime Timeline events?** No

When researching a claim that could draw on Kelvin timeline evidence, flag it explicitly: "This is established in the Kelvin timeline and does not apply to the Prime Timeline."

---

## Tier C — Animated Series (Current Working Status: Canon)

- **TAS**: See above. Now generally treated as canon.
- **Lower Decks**: Producer-confirmed as canon, set in the Prime Timeline post-Nemesis era.
- **Prodigy**: Producer-confirmed as canon.

---

## Tier D — Expanded Universe (Not Screen Canon)

**Memory Beta domain.** These require explicit disclosure when cited.

- Novels (Pocket Books and others)
- Comics (IDW Publishing, Marvel, DC, others)
- Games (Star Trek Online, etc.)
- RPG sourcebooks
- Fan fiction

Relevant for:
- Understanding how a concept was developed before reaching screen
- Identifying whether something originated in licensed fiction
- Giving the user more material than screen canon alone provides (when they ask for it)

## Common Canon Boundary Disputes

**"Did Spock prime's Romulus destruction establish the mining colony timeline or was it already happening?"**
→ Addressed in ST 2009 and the Countdown comic (Tier D, not canon). The film itself is Tier A; the comic backstory is not.

**"Is Section 31 a retcon?"**
→ First introduced in DS9 "Inquisition" (6x18). All prior appearances of Section 31 in series set before DS9's era (ENT, DIS) are either retcon or the section existed covertly for longer than canon established. Both readings are defensible.

**"Is the holodeck a weapon?"**
→ Philosophical, not a canon boundary question. But the Prime Directive implications of holodeck simulations of real people are not addressed by canon.

**"What stardate is this episode set in?"**
→ Stardates are internally inconsistent across TOS, TNG, and later series. They are canon (in the sense that characters use them), but they are not a reliable chronological tool. Production order and airdate order sometimes diverge. Memory Alpha's stardate timeline is the best working reference.

---

## Writer/Producer Statements vs. What Aired

When a writer says "we intended X" but the episode shows Y, **what aired is canon**. Intent is production context (Tier 2); it illuminates why something is the way it is but does not overwrite what's on screen.

Exception: When a line of dialogue was cut from an aired episode, the cut dialogue is not canon even if the writer cites it.
